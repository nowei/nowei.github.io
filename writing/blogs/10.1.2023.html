10/1/2023: On Time and Moving On

It's 1:12 AM.

I woke up yesterday to my dad asking me if I wanted to go with him on a hike, but I was too tired from staying up to play games with some friends. The good, healthy thing probably would've been to join him anyways, regardless of how tired I was. I'm tired every day and I have to do things every day anyways, so why shouldn't I go? I went back to sleep, telling him that I'd go next week. But what will change about next week? Will I not feel tired on a Saturday morning? It's hard to say, but how many times can we kick things to the next week before there is no next week?

Eventually time catches up to us and there is no more next time.

An aside: Maybe part of the problem is that I'm tired every day. But even if I'm tired, I still have to do things. I still wake up to volunteer (albeit consistently a bit late). I still wake up to work or go to work. I still wake up to greet the day, day after day. Maybe I avoid waking up because I subconsciously associate waking up with the expectations and responsibilities of living in a society. Although I'm probably not the only one who avoids waking up because revenge bedtime procrastination is a thing. I'd probably be a lot less tired if I just slept earlier or exercised regularly. Although it's hard to live for the future when the present seems so bleak. Maybe the world is getting better, but it seems like every step forward is hard-fought and suffers the risk of getting set back multiple steps. How do we effect change in a society that is scared of the future? Shirking away from all the possibilities for the future because some outcomes may be bad? Have we become so pessimistic that we are driven to inaction for fear of what action could bring? We probably maintain the status quo because that is what is easy, not because it is what is right.

It is always fun meeting up with people, friends, and acquaintances. Although when it gets to the time to go, there is always a certain bittersweet feeling. I pretty much always say something along the lines of, "until next time," as a goodbye since there are no good goodbyes. Then we may not talk for days, days that turn into weeks, weeks that turn into months, and then months that turn into years. There may not actually be a next time, who knows what'll happen in the future? Like friends or coworkers who say that you should stay in touch and you just don't. It's not like you actually dislike the other person in any sense of the word or that you don't actually want to stay in touch, but life gets in the way. We live our lives from moment to moment. Then we look back, thinking where the time went. At times it feels like time is ephemeral, and at other times it may feel agonizingly long. There is no time. Yet we have all the time in the world (or at least the time granted to us by the genetic lottery, luck, and maybe some margin of error (read: wiggle room) based on some measure of self-care).

In the universe of people, this probably isn't an uncommon occurrence. There is nothing fundamentally wrong with this because every person is their own, with their own thoughts and living their own lives with autonomy or at least however much autonomy is granted to them in the system we exist and participate in. (Because in reality we are only living our lives in a fashion that is begrudgingly accepted by the oppressors, with every comfort and right granted to us through collective action. Also note that rights and opportunities for some groups is still unequal because some things about society are fundamentally broken.) Relationships are a two-way street that requires both parties to maintain lest it falls into disrepair. We weave in and out of each others lives, so how do you make these interactions meaningful? Not pointless? How can we spend our time together such that it moves the metaphorical needle? The people we spend every day with may not always be around, so how can we derive meaning from our interactions?

Everyone probably lives with some sort of existential angst, but maybe it is just more at the forefront for some than others. Or they distract themselves with life keeping busy such that they don't need to think about it? The sense of existential dread hanging at the back of everyone's mind. "What am I doing?" "Who am I?" "Am I enough? What does that even mean?"

I got home from Seattle a few hours ago from a friend's going away party.

If they ever read this, however unlikely, "Hi! xD"

It's not like they'll be gone forever or that I'd never see them again out in this vast world. Will things fundamentally change? Probably? I spent a lot of time with them in college (per aspera ad astra and whatnot) and saw them like once a week for the last couple of years and it was always nice or good to spend time with them. So this goodbye was especially bittersweet, but people must move on. They must move on to the next chapter of their lives, to tackle bigger, better things. There is generally no guarantee that things will always be better or that life will be good, as life will always have its mix of ups and downs. Sometimes all we can do is hope that nothing goes wrong in this system that is barely held together by duct tape and dreams.

I'd never tell them this in person, but they're probably one of the best people I know. Not because they're particularly good at everything they do (which is also not to say that they aren't good at every thing they try), but because of how real they are. I've interacted with this person over the years, saw them obtain hobbies that came and went and came back again, worked with them and watched them learn new things; essentially change and grow as a person. People change all the time, but it's interesting to reflect on all the changes over time. In a few words, they are chaotic, strong-willed, and raw. They actively look for new experiences or excuses to try new things and dragged people along with her. They are someone that pushes people up and forward rather than beat them down.

It has historically been unsaid and there have been many times when I have said a last farewell (sometimes unknowingly), but I hope that people know that even if I never see or speak to them again in the rest of our short lives, I am rooting for them. Come what may with expectation and ambition, wherever you are or whatever you're trying to accomplish, I am rooting for you. To be human and mortal is to struggle and burn like a star against the pitch-black void that just wants to extinguish all light and life.

May we meet in better times and better places. Until next time.
